\begin{abstract}
The central contribution of this paper is a single representational move: rather than introducing a separate primitive for \emph{assertions}, we represent them as ordinary posits over reserved roles. This uniformity lets every opinion, retraction, and correction be expressed by the same construct as the facts they concern, enabling recursive meta-commentary without new machinery. Building on this, \emph{transitional representation} treats concurrency (multiple sources and perspectives), bitemporality (what was believed when, about what happened when), and uncertainty (graded confidence and explicit disagreement) as aspects of one primitive: the \emph{posit}, an \(n\)-ary, time-stamped statement.

Existing representation formalisms typically handle at most one or two of these dimensions without either losing provenance or forcing early conflict resolution. We formalize a class layer in the same terms, showing that model-level distinctions --- including disputed classifications and parallel model frameworks maintained by different positors --- require no separate mechanism. Deterministic temporal-resolution procedures are given for both the posit and the assertion layer; the familiar global \emph{information in effect} appears as the special case induced by assertion posits. A reference query language, Traqula, illustrates how the formalism is realized in an executable system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\noindent
Conventional databases can store facts, but they often discard the circumstances under which those facts were stated: who stated them, how confident they were, when the statement was recorded, and how both the world and opinions about it change over time. Modeling these requirements explicitly in conventional designs is complex and therefore often omitted, producing lossy implementations and lost provenance \cite{GolfarelliRizzi}. Yet in practice, statements have origins and degrees of confidence \cite{Liu}, and many domains require full auditability and non-destructive history (e.g., regulated finance, clinical records, intelligence and judicial applications) \cite{FisherEtAl}.

\noindent
Even when temporal support exists at the database level, it is typically layered onto the relational model and remains insufficient for representing multi-source disagreement and evolving beliefs \cite{SQL2011,Johnston}. More broadly, conventional systems tend to demand conformance too early: data must already fit a table, key, type, and integration regime before it can be stored. Schema-light systems relax these demands, but often shift the burden to readers who must reconstruct meaning, consistency, and provenance afterwards. A central motivation in this paper is to move conflict handling from \emph{write time} (where integration rules prematurely discard alternatives) to \emph{query time} (where decisions can be made using the full body of competing evidence) \cite{MassaroEtAl,LinEtAl}, while still allowing classifications, and ultimately richer schema-level commitments, to emerge as represented information rather than as a single fixed prerequisite for storage.

\noindent
We use the following running example throughout:
\begin{quote}
\small
The accused, Archie, was seen fleeing the scene of the crime by two witnesses, Donna and Charlie. Donna is certain that the accused was clean shaved, while Charlie thinks he had a red beard. When the victim, Bella, later regained consciousness, she corroborated Charlie's story, at which time Donna retracted her statement. In fact, Archie had been wearing a fake red beard during the attack, but it fell off when he fled, eventually leading to his conviction through a DNA match.
\end{quote}

\noindent
\textsc{Contributions.} The contributions of this paper are: (i) posits as the single atomic representation construct, with assertions treated as ordinary posits over reserved roles, (ii) a compact class layer showing how model-level distinctions --- including subclass relationships, parallel model frameworks, and model-level disagreement --- are represented inside the same formalism, (iii) a signed certainty scale \(c\in[-1,1]\), and (iv) deterministic temporal-resolution procedures that support both ordinary posit cuts and the global \emph{information in effect} slice for assertion bodies, as well as clause-local temporal cuts chosen at query time. Richer typing and full constraint enforcement are deferred to future work. Identification remains outside the representation layer by design.

\section{The Transitional Model (Core Constructs)}
\subsection{Posits}
\label{Sec:posits}
\deffy{def. of an appearance}{%
An \emph{appearance} is a pair \((i, r)\) where the first element is a unique identifier and the second a string called \emph{role}.}{appearance}

\noindent
\textbf{Note on identifiers.} Identifiers are opaque tokens: the formalism receives them as the \emph{outcome} of an identification process that operates entirely outside the representation layer. Identification is a search process by which circumstances are matched against available data, ending in one of two outcomes: an identity is established or it is not. What those circumstances are may vary widely --- from a reproducible customer-number lookup to an open-ended detective investigation. When the process is so reproducible that the same circumstances always produce the same result, those circumstances constitute a \emph{key}. Keys are useful automation aids for the identification process, but they must never affect the representation model itself: a model that encodes a particular identification process would reflect a single point of view, be bound to the time frame in which that process is valid, and risk obsolescence when the process changes.

\noindent
Identifiers are also the one element that positors must share for disagreement to be interpretable as disagreement about the same referent. Two positors can only disagree if their assertions reference the same appearance set. How such shared reference is established is an identification problem outside the representation layer. Everything else concerning an entity remains expressible \emph{within} the formalism as ordinary posits with reserved roles: classification (``J42 is a person since 1980-02-13''), class transitions (``J42 is a female child'' until ``J42 is a female adult''), and even conflicting co-reference hypotheses (``J42 and K99 refer to the same individual'', asserted with some certainty by one positor and disputed by another) are all posits subject to the usual assertion mechanics. Such co-reference posits are selected by the same bitemporal rules as any other posit, but traversing them to expand a query from \(J42\) to \(K99\) is a consumer-level policy decision, not part of the core \emph{information in effect} operator.

\deffy{def. of an appearance set}{%
An \emph{appearance set}, \(\{(i_1, r_1), \ldots (i_n, r_n)\}\), is a set of appearances where any role \(r_i\) may only appear once.}{appearanceset}

\deffy{def. of a posit}{%
A \emph{posit}~\cite{Oxford} is a triple, \(\left[D, v, t_{\mathrm{app}}\right]\), where the first element is an appearance set, the second a data value, and the third a time point. The domain \(\mathbb{t}\) from which time points are taken is called \emph{appearance time}.}{posit}

\noindent
\textbf{Interpretation.} A posit states that an appearance set \(D\) has value \(v\) from (and including) appearance time \(t_{\mathrm{app}}\) onward, until another posit with the same appearance set becomes applicable. Appearance sets are permanent anchors of change: they remain in the body of information indefinitely, even after the entities they reference no longer exist, because history must remain queryable.

\noindent
Appearance sets of size one act like \emph{properties}; appearance sets of size greater than one act like \emph{relationships}. For example:
\begin{align*}
[\{(A, \textit{has beard})\}, \textrm{fluffy red}, \textrm{10:00}] \\
[\{(A, \textit{has beard})\}, \textrm{shaved clean}, \textrm{10:02}]
\end{align*}

\noindent
and, for a relationship:
\begin{align*}
[\{(A, \textit{is accused}), (B, \textit{is victim}), (C, \textit{is witness})\}, \textrm{at scene of crime}, \textrm{09:58}] \\
[\{(A, \textit{is accused}), (B, \textit{is victim}), (C, \textit{is witness})\}, \textrm{dispersed}, \textrm{10:02}]
\end{align*}

\noindent
\textbf{Why posits are not enough.} Corrections cannot be modeled adequately as additional posits alone. If a birth date was recorded incorrectly, one might add a later posit with a different value. However, posits form an unordered set: there is no built-in mechanism for one posit to override another. When two posits share the same appearance set and appearance time but carry different values, the collection is ambiguous---there is no way, from inside the set of posits alone, to determine which corresponds to the actual state of affairs.\footnote{Think of posits as a shuffled deck of cards handed to you; you must identify the true ones using only the information printed on the cards themselves---no card can designate itself as the authoritative one.} If interpreted as a natural transition, conflicting same-time posits would imply that Archie was born in August \emph{and} in September. Corrections must therefore be handled at a different level, by expressing evolving \emph{opinions} about posits rather than by adding rival posits.

\subsection{Assertions as Posits}
\noindent
Posits are treated as syntactical items towards which different actors may hold different and evolving opinions. To bring posits meaning while keeping a uniform representation, assertions are represented as posits themselves---meta-posits that bind a posited statement to a \emph{positor}, a signed certainty, and an assertion time.

\deffy{def. of an assertion}{%
An \emph{assertion} is a posit on the form
$$\left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, c, T_{\mathrm{ast}}\right]$$
where \(p_{id}\) identifies the posit being asserted, \(s_{id}\) identifies the \emph{positor} (source), \(T_{\mathrm{ast}}\in\mathbb{T}\) is \emph{assertion time}, and \(c\in[-1,1]\) is \emph{signed certainty}.}{assertion}

\noindent
Representing assertions as posits preserves uniformity: because an assertion is itself a posit, it can in turn be asserted by another positor, enabling recursive layers of meta-commentary—uncertainty about uncertainty, provenance of provenance—within the same construct.

\noindent
Intuitively, \(c=1\) means the positor is certain the posit holds, \(c=0\) means complete uncertainty, and \(c=-1\) means the positor is certain the posit does \emph{not} hold.

\noindent
Signed certainty can be read in two complementary ways: either as a number attached to a posit, or as a way to refer to the \emph{complement} of a posit (e.g., ``not shaved clean''). Complements are convenient for exposition, while signed certainty is convenient for storage and algebraic manipulation.

\noindent
In the running example, let:
\begin{alignat*}{3}
p_{1} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{fluffy red},\;&& \textrm{10:00}] \\
p_{2} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{shaved clean},\;&& \textrm{10:02}] \\
[\{(p_{1}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
[\{(p_{2}, \textit{posit}), (D, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]
\end{alignat*}

\subsection{A Minimal Class Layer}
\deffy{def. of a class and a classifier}{%
Reserve the roles \textit{named}, \textit{thing}, and \textit{class}. A \emph{class} is an identifier \(c_{id}\) for which some unary posit \([\{(c_{id}, \textit{named})\}, n, t]\) exists, where \(n\) is the name of the class. A \emph{classifier} is a posit \([\{(i,\textit{thing}), (c_{id},\textit{class})\}, a, t]\) relating an identifier \(i\) to a class identifier \(c_{id}\), where \(a\) is a marker such as \textit{active} or \textit{inactive}.}{class}

\noindent
This layer shows that a minimal amount of model structure can be expressed inside the same formalism as first-order information. In the running example, let:
\begin{alignat*}{3}
q_{1} = [\{(C_{\mathrm{person}}, \textit{named})\},\;&& \textit{Person},\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
q_{2} = [\{(A, \textit{thing}), (C_{\mathrm{person}}, \textit{class})\},\;&& \textit{active},\;&& \textrm{1972\text{-}08\text{-}20}] \\
q_{3} = [\{(C_{\mathrm{suspect}}, \textit{named})\},\;&& \textit{Suspect},\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
q_{4} = [\{(A, \textit{thing}), (C_{\mathrm{suspect}}, \textit{class})\},\;&& \textit{active},\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
[\{(q_{1}, \textit{posit}), (M, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
[\{(q_{2}, \textit{posit}), (M, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
[\{(q_{3}, \textit{posit}), (I, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
[\{(q_{4}, \textit{posit}), (I, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]
\end{alignat*}

\noindent
Here \(q_{1}\) and \(q_{3}\) name two classes, while \(q_{2}\) says that Archie (\(A\)) belongs to the class \textit{Person} from his birth onward and \(q_{4}\) says that Archie belongs to the class \textit{Suspect} from 2024-03-01 onward. The two claims live at different levels of abstraction, may be asserted by different positors, and are queryable and revisable like any other posits. The same mechanism also supports conflicting classifications. One positor may assert that Archie is a \textit{Suspect} with certainty \(1\), while another asserts the same classifier with certainty \(0\), or instead asserts a competing classifier such as \textit{Witness} or \textit{Victim}. Such disagreement is represented in exactly the same way as disagreement about beard state or event participation.

\noindent
A query over the information in effect can therefore treat class memberships and domain facts uniformly: ``Who was classified as a suspect on 2024-03-01?'' is handled by the same retrieval machinery as ``Who was observed with a beard at 10:00?''

\noindent
\textbf{Subclass relationships.}
Because a class identifier is itself just an identifier, it can appear in the \textit{thing} role of a classifier posit. To express that \textit{Suspect} is a subclass of \textit{Person}, one posits:
\begin{align*}
q_{5} = [\{(C_{\mathrm{suspect}}, \textit{thing}), (C_{\mathrm{person}}, \textit{class})\}, \textit{active}, \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]
\end{align*}
asserting that the class identifier $C_{\mathrm{suspect}}$ belongs to the class $C_{\mathrm{person}}$. This is an ordinary classifier posit with a class identifier in the \textit{thing} role. Hierarchies of arbitrary depth arise by composing such posits without introducing any new construct. Like any other posit, subclass relationships have an appearance time and can be asserted, retracted, or disputed.

\noindent
\textbf{Parallel model frameworks.}
Because classifiers are asserted posits, an entire classification schema belongs to whichever positors assert its components. Two positors may maintain entirely distinct classification frameworks over the same identifiers without conflict. Suppose a medical records system (positor $M$) classifies patients by clinical criteria (\textit{Person}, \textit{Patient}, \textit{Vaccinated}), while an insurance system (positor $B$) classifies the same identifiers by billing status (\textit{Insured}, \textit{SelfPay}, \textit{Exempt}). Neither schema is globally privileged: both co-exist as separate sets of assertion posits. A query can retrieve $M$'s classifications, $B$'s classifications, or both together---applying the same bitemporal \emph{information in effect} operator uniformly to each. There is no integration ceremony at write time, and no global schema needs to be agreed upon in advance.

\noindent
This also means that classification \emph{frameworks} themselves evolve and can be disputed. If $M$ restructures its schema by splitting \textit{Patient} into \textit{AcutePatient} and \textit{ChronicPatient} at some point in time, that restructuring is recorded exactly as it happens: old classifiers carry one history, new classifiers carry another, all queryable bitemporally. The model-level change enters the same information store as the object-level data it governs.

\noindent
\textbf{Model-level disagreement.}
The relationship between assertions and classification yields a further important consequence: the same mechanism that allows Donna and Charlie to disagree about beard state also allows different positors to disagree about classification. One investigator might classify Archie as \textit{Suspect} with certainty $1.0$; his defense counsel might assert the same classifier posit with certainty $-1.0$ (certainly not a suspect) and instead assert a \textit{Witness} classifier with certainty $0.75$. This is model-level disagreement, expressed identically to object-level disagreement. No special machinery is needed for ``we disagree about which model applies.''

\noindent
Class-level disagreement is resolvable at query time by the same \emph{information in effect} selection used for domain facts. Two queries with different resolution policies---say, one that trusts the investigator and one that trusts the defense counsel---will produce different effective classification frameworks, yet draw on the same underlying body of information without modification.

\noindent
\textbf{Expressivity of the minimal layer.}
The class layer as defined supports: class naming, class membership, subclass relationships by chaining, class transitions over time (a later classifier posit supersedes an earlier one for the same \textit{thing}/\textit{class} pair), and disputed membership or hierarchy at any level. It does not natively support class disjointness constraints (nothing prevents the same identifier from simultaneously belonging to classes that a domain rule would deem mutually exclusive), cardinality constraints (a positor can freely classify as many identifiers under one class as they wish), or closed-world assumptions (the absence of a classifier posit implies nothing about non-membership). These limitations belong to the representation layer by design: richer constraints and policies are the province of a schema and constraint layer that can itself be represented as posits in the same formalism, deferring enforcement to query time or to a dedicated validation pass.

\section{Epistemic Properties of the Model}
\noindent
\textbf{Imprecision versus uncertainty.} Two distinct concepts govern how a posit relates to the world. \emph{Imprecision} is a property of values: a value such as \textit{fluffy red}, \textit{approximately noon}, or \textit{Person} applies to a range of possible realities rather than to one perfectly delimited state. Yet when a positor makes an assertion, that imprecision is evaluated \emph{crisply}: from the positor's point of view the actual beard color either counts as \textit{fluffy red} or it does not; the observed time either counts as \textit{approximately noon} or it does not. \emph{Uncertainty}, by contrast, is subjective and contextual: it is the degree of belief a positor holds towards the posit after making that evaluation, given their own information and viewpoint.

\noindent
This distinction locates fuzziness in the assertion rather than in the value itself. A positor may be completely certain about an imprecise value (asserting \(c=1\) for ``approximately noon'' without knowing the exact minute) and completely uncertain about a precise one (asserting \(c=0\) for an exact timestamp heard second-hand). Increasing precision may even reduce certainty: someone may be sure that Archie wore a red beard, less sure that it was \emph{fluffy red}, and less sure still that it was \emph{fluffy red at 10{:}00 sharp}. Signed certainty therefore measures subjective commitment to a posit, not objective probability in the world. This is why certainty may be negative: the positor is certain of the \emph{complement}, not assigning a negative probability to reality.

\noindent
This separation preserves the law of excluded middle at the level of evaluation. For any specific subject in a specific context, a value either applies or it does not; what remains open is not truth in that moment of evaluation but the subject's confidence in the posit they have formed. Transitional representation thus treats values as \emph{crisp but imprecise} and assertions as the locus of subjective doubt.

This limitation is acceptable for the representation layer because the immediate task is faithful preservation of what each source asserted. \subsection{Well-formed bodies of information}
\deffy{def. of a body of information}{%
A \emph{body of information} is a set of posits.}{body}

\noindent
In the remainder of this subsection, and in the immediately following definitions that refer to the information in effect, the bodies under discussion are bodies of assertion posits. Such a body is \emph{well-formed} when its assertions satisfy a set of mutually reinforcing properties. The first ensures that negated values and negative certainties are interchangeable:

\deffy{def. of symmetric}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{symmetric} iff the assertion posits \([\{(p,\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},-c,T]\) and \([\{(\bar{p},\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},c,T]\) are equivalent for a posit \(p\) and its complement \(\bar{p}\).}{symmetry}

\noindent
Symmetry allows a positor to express ``certainly not fluffy red'' either as certainty \(-1\) for \(p\!=\)[\ldots, \textit{fluffy red}, \ldots] or as certainty \(1\) for \(\bar{p}\!=\)[\ldots, \textit{not fluffy red}, \ldots]. To avoid storing complemented values, any symmetrical body can be normalized:

\deffy{def. of canonical}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{canonical} iff all assertions are made against posits with non-negated values.}{canonical}

\noindent
The next property bounds the certainties for a value and its complement so they cannot independently saturate the scale:

\deffy{def. of bounded}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{bounded} iff the certainties in the assertion posits \([\{(p,\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},c,T]\) and \([\{(\bar{p},\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},d,T]\) satisfy \(c + d \in \{-1, 0, 1\}\) (equivalently, the fixed-point identity \(\abs{c + d} = \abs{c + d}^2\) holds).}{boundary}

\noindent
For example, if Charlie asserts \(c = 0.75\) for ``fluffy red'', boundary implies \(d = 0.25\) for ``not fluffy red''. The symmetry and bounded conditions on the certainty scale are illustrated in Figure~\ref{Figure:boundsym}. The next property prevents a positor from simultaneously holding two different certainties for the very same posit:

\deffy{def. of exclusive}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{exclusive} iff no two assertions by the same positor for the same posit differ only in certainty.}{exclusive}

\noindent
Exclusivity rules out statements such as ``I think Archie had a red beard and I am also sure he had a red beard''. Even though the certainties \(-0.25\) and \(0.75\) for a posit are equivalent via symmetry, only one should persist. The final individual property requires that all positors share the same interpretation of appearances:

\deffy{def. of universal}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{universal} iff positors agree on all appearances.}{universal}

\noindent
Without universality, disagreement cannot be interpreted as disagreement about the same referent. Operationally, universality is satisfied when positors share identifiers through a common identification process: if two positors both reference the appearance \((J42, \textit{has beard})\) because they obtained the identifier \(J42\) through the same lookup, they are already universal with respect to that appearance. This connects universality to the Note on Identifiers in Section~\ref{Sec:posits} --- shared reference is an identification problem, not a representational one. A body in which positors share some but not all identifiers is not ill-formed in any deeper sense; the well-formedness properties simply apply only where shared reference exists, leaving unshared appearances outside any cross-positor comparison. Together, the four properties yield:

\deffy{def. of comprehensive}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{comprehensive} iff it is symmetric, canonical, bounded, exclusive, and universal.}{comprehensive}

\noindent
From here on all bodies of information are assumed to be comprehensive. In a concrete implementation such as Positorium~\cite{Positorium}, these properties serve as integrity constraints: the system normalises symmetric statements into canonical form and rejects assertions that would violate exclusivity or boundary rules. They can equally be applied as diagnostic criteria to audit an existing body of information. Two further properties pertain to the \emph{information in effect} rather than to individual assertions:

\deffy{def. of decisive and indecisive}{%
When multiple assertions by the same positor are in effect for posits with the same appearance set but different values, that positor is \emph{indecisive} with respect to that appearance set; when exactly one such assertion is in effect, the positor is \emph{decisive}. A body of information is \emph{decisive} if no indecisive positor exists in any information in effect, and \emph{indecisive} otherwise.}{decisiveness}

\noindent
A traditional relational database is always decisive: it permits at most one value per attribute at any given time. In transitional representation, indecisiveness is a valid and often informative state. A decisive body is always non-contradictory; in the general case, the following inequality must hold:

\deffy{def. of non-contradictory}{%
A body of information is said to be \emph{non-contradictory} iff for every information in effect the certainties across all concurrent assertions by the same positor for the same appearance set satisfy
$$|\{i \mid c_i < 0\}| + \sum_{i=1}^{n}c_i\leq 1,$$
where $|\{i \mid c_i < 0\}|$ counts the number of negative certainties in the set.}{noncontradiction}

\noindent
\textsc{Derivation.} The formula follows from the requirement that the total \emph{positive commitment} across mutually exclusive alternatives must not exceed~1. For a positive certainty $c_i > 0$, the positor claims $c_i$ units of certainty that value $v_i$ holds. For a negative certainty $c_j < 0$, the positor claims $|c_j|$ certainty that $v_j$ does \emph{not} hold; by the bounded property this implicitly allocates $1 - |c_j| = 1 + c_j$ units of certainty toward the set of all other alternatives. Summing both types of commitment gives:
$$\sum_{c_j < 0}(1 + c_j) + \sum_{c_i > 0}c_i = |\{i \mid c_i < 0\}| + \sum_{i=1}^{n}c_i.$$
Requiring this total not to exceed~1 prevents the positor from collectively over-committing across mutually exclusive alternatives. When all certainties are non-negative the formula reduces to the classical budget constraint $\sum c_i \leq 1$; asserting $c_j=-1$ (``certainly not $v_j$'') contributes zero to the budget (since $1 + (-1) = 0$), so a positor may freely disbelieve any number of values without tightening the constraint on their positive claims. When all certainties are non-negative and sum to exactly~1, the positor has distributed their total commitment fully across the alternatives, recovering the classical probability simplex: each $c_i$ can be read as the probability assigned to $v_i$.

\noindent
\textsc{Example.} Suppose Donna has two assertions simultaneously in effect at \((\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}, \textrm{10:03})\):
\[a_5 = [\{(p_4,\textit{posit}),(D,\textit{ascertains})\},0.25,\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}]\quad\text{and}\quad a_6 = [\{(p_2,\textit{posit}),(D,\textit{ascertains})\},-0.75,\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}]\]
(``possibly fluffy red'' and ``probably not shaved clean''). One certainty is negative ($-0.75$), and the sum is $0.25 + (-0.75) = -0.5$, so the left-hand side equals $1 + (-0.5) = 0.5 \leq 1$. Donna is well within bounds and is not contradicting herself. Note that a positor may simultaneously assert \(c=-1\) (``certainly not'') for an unlimited number of posits without ever violating the inequality.

\noindent
\textsc{Mutual exclusivity.} The non-contradiction formula applies per appearance set and assumes that the posits being compared represent \emph{mutually exclusive} alternatives for a single value slot. ``Archie's beard is fluffy red'' and ``Archie's beard is shaved clean'' are alternatives for the same appearance set \(\{(A, \textit{has beard})\}\), so they compete. If a domain requires multi-valued attributes, each value dimension must be separated into distinct roles so that each appearance set carries exactly one value at a time --- the functional dependency from appearance set to value is a design requirement, not an incidental restriction. For example, ``Archie speaks English'' and ``Archie speaks French'' should not be modeled as competing values of a single \textit{language} slot. They are better represented as distinct appearance sets such as \(\{(A,\textit{person}),(E,\textit{language})\}\) and \(\{(A,\textit{person}),(F,\textit{language})\}\), each with a value such as \textit{fluent} and its own appearance time. The contradiction budget then applies independently to each person-language pair.

\section{Temporal Resolution}
\subsection{Retractions and corrections}
\deffy{def. of complete uncertainty}{%
An assertion posit is called \emph{completely uncertain} when the signed certainty is $0.0$.}{uncertain}

\deffy{def. of a retraction}{%
Let \(\mathbb{A}\) be a body of information. A \emph{retraction} is an assertion posit
\(\left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, 0.0, T'\right] \in \mathbb{A}\)
for which there exists an earlier assertion
\(\left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, c, T\right] \in \mathbb{A}\)
with \(c\neq 0\) and \(T < T'\).}{retraction}

\deffy{def. of a correction}{%
Let \(\mathbb{A}\) be a body of information. The three assertion posits
\(a_1 = \left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, c, T\right]\),
\(a_2 = \left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, 0.0, T'\right]\), and
\(a_3 = \left[\{(p'_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, c', T'\right]\)
form a \emph{correction} when \(p_{id} \neq p'_{id}\), \(c\neq 0\), \(c'\neq 0\), and \(T < T'\).}{correction}

\subsection{Resolution of posits and assertions}
\noindent
\textsc{Selection assumptions.} For the purposes of retrieval, any temporal domain used by a query is assumed to be totally ordered. This assumption concerns only the ordering required by the selection procedure. It does not deny that temporal expressions encountered in source information may be imprecise; rather, such imprecision must be evaluated, normalised, or otherwise made comparable before selection is applied.

\deffy{def. of posit resolution}{%
Let \(\mathbb{P}\) be a set of posits \([D,v,t]\) over an ordered temporal domain. The \emph{resolution} of \(\mathbb{P}\) at a cut point \(t_@\) is the subset
\(\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_@) \subset \mathbb{P}\)
containing exactly those posits with \(t \leq t_@\) whose time \(t\) is maximal for their appearance set \(D\). If several posits for the same appearance set share that maximal time, they are all retained.}{resolution}

\noindent
This single-axis rule is the generic temporal treatment of posits. It applies to ordinary domain posits, class posits, and any other posit family whose own time coordinate is the one being queried. A useful information store may therefore exist without assertions at all; in that case, temporal querying is simply repeated application of \(\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_@)\) to the relevant posit families.

\deffy{def. of the information in effect}{%
Let \(\mathbb{A}\) be a body of information whose members are assertion posits
\(\left[\{(p_{id}, \textit{posit}), (s_{id}, \textit{ascertains})\}, c, T\right]\)
about posits \(p = [D, v, t_{\mathrm{app}}]\). The \emph{information in effect} at \((T_@, t_@) \in \mathbb{T} \times \mathbb{t}\) is the subset
\(\mathbb{A}(T_@, t_@) \subset \mathbb{A}\)
found using the following criteria:
\begin{criteria}
\item Let \(\mathbb{A}_1 \subset \mathbb{A}\) be the assertions satisfying \(T \leq T_@\) and \(t_{\mathrm{app}} \leq t_@\).
\item Let \(\mathbb{A}_2 \subset \mathbb{A}_1\) be the assertions with \(c \neq 0\) and the latest appearance time \(t_{\mathrm{app}}\) for each combination of \(s_{id}\) and \(D\), excluding those for which a retraction exists at some \(T_0\) with \(T \leq T_0 \leq T_@\).
\item Let \(\mathbb{A}(T_@, t_@) \subset \mathbb{A}_2\) be the assertions from \(\mathbb{A}_2\) with the latest assertion time \(T\) for each combination of \(s_{id}\), \(D\), and \(v\).
\end{criteria}}{effect}

\noindent
In less formal terms, ordinary posit resolution keeps the latest visible posit per appearance set at the chosen cut point. Assertions require one additional layer: first rewind to assertions not after \((T_@, t_@)\); then keep, per positor and appearance set, the latest asserted appearance-time state that has not been retracted; finally keep the latest assertion-time version for each value. Bitemporality is therefore not a universal prerequisite of the formalism. It arises specifically when one resolves assertion posits about temporally indexed posits.

\noindent
\textsc{Query-dependent cuts.} A query need not use one global cut for every clause. A query processor may resolve one family of posits at \(t_1\), another at \(t_2\), and an assertion body at \((T_3,t_3)\), then join the resulting subsets afterwards. The choice of how many cuts to use, and how they are related, is a query-time decision. In richer cases a cut used in one clause may itself be obtained from another resolved posit, so that temporal resolution becomes data-dependent while the local rules remain unchanged.

\noindent
Such data-dependent cuts are well-defined under a stratified query discipline in which each cut may depend only on literals or on bindings produced by earlier clauses. Circular cut dependencies are therefore excluded at the query-language level rather than by the representation formalism itself.

\noindent
\textsc{Proposition (determinism).} Under the ordering assumption above, both \(\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_@)\) and \(\mathbb{A}(T_@, t_@)\) are uniquely determined for every admissible input and cut point. Consequently, any query that combines finitely many such cuts is deterministic once its temporal bindings are fixed.

\noindent
\textsc{Proof sketch.} For ordinary posits, \(\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_@)\) is a deterministic filter followed by grouped maxima on appearance time, with ties retained rather than broken arbitrarily. For assertion bodies, Step 1 is a deterministic filter using only order comparisons. Step 2 deterministically retains exactly those non-retracted assertions whose appearance time is maximal within each \((s_{id}, D)\) group; if several values share that same maximal appearance time, they are all retained, so indecisiveness is preserved rather than resolved arbitrarily. Step 3 deterministically retains, within each \((s_{id}, D, v)\) group, the assertions with maximal assertion time. Because each step is deterministic and no step introduces arbitrary tie-breaking, the resulting subsets are unique. A query that combines several temporal cuts remains deterministic provided that the temporal points supplied to each invocation are themselves determined by literals or by earlier resolved bindings.

\noindent
\textsc{Implementation note.} The procedures above are constructive semantic descriptions, not prescribed physical execution plans. One invocation of \(\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_@)\) reduces retrieval to temporal filtering followed by grouped maxima over appearance sets, yielding linear cost in the number of temporally admissible candidates under standard hashing assumptions. One invocation of \(\mathbb{A}(T_@, t_@)\) reduces retrieval to temporal filtering followed by grouped maxima first on \((s_{id}, D)\) and then on \((s_{id}, D, v)\), again linear in the surviving assertion set under the same assumptions. In a practical store, the same semantics can be supported by indexes on the relevant time coordinates and appearance-set signatures. A query with temporally dependent joins simply invokes the relevant operator repeatedly, possibly with cut points supplied by earlier clauses; in the naive case, the cost of such a clause therefore grows linearly in its candidate set for each intermediate binding carried into it. The exact cost therefore depends on storage design, selectivity, and the number of clause-local resolutions performed. Because the stored information is finite and explicit, obtaining resolved slices does not require recursive inference, global fixpoint computation, or theorem proving.

\noindent
\textsc{Temporally dependent joins.} Because temporal resolution is defined independently of any particular posit type, the same rule can be applied uniformly to domain facts, class assertions, and assertions about assertions. This matters when one clause determines the cut used in another. For example, a query may first resolve a marriage posit at the present time to obtain its marriage time \(m_t\), then resolve assertions about that posit \emph{as of} \(m_t\), and finally resolve names for the spouses without requiring that every clause share the same global temporal cut. The formalism therefore separates \emph{how} a body is temporally resolved from \emph{how many} such resolutions a query combines.

\noindent
\textsc{Worked example.} Consider three beard-related posits and three assertions:
\begin{alignat*}{3}
p_{1} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{fluffy red},\;&& \textrm{10:00}]  \\
p_{2} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{shaved clean},\;&& \textrm{10:02}]  \\
p_{3} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{shaved clean},\;&& \textrm{10:00}]  \\
a_1 = [\{(p_{2}, \textit{posit}), (D, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
a_2 = [\{(p_{1}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
a_3 = [\{(p_{3}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& -1.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]
\end{alignat*}

\noindent
With \(\mathbb{A}=\{a_1,a_2,a_3\}\), the information in effect at \((\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}02}, \textrm{10:01})\) is \(\{a_2, a_3\}\) (Donna's statement concerns \(t_{\mathrm{app}}=10{:}02\), which is after 10:01). At \((\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}02}, \textrm{10:03})\), the information in effect is \(\{a_1,a_2,a_3\}\).

\noindent
Now suppose Bella wakes up on 2024-03-03 and Donna retracts her earlier certainty while tentatively aligning with Charlie:
\begin{alignat*}{3}
p_{4} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{fluffy red},\;&& \textrm{10:02}] \\
a_4 = [\{(p_{2}, \textit{posit}), (D, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.0,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}] \\
a_5 = [\{(p_{4}, \textit{posit}), (D, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.25,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}]
\end{alignat*}

\noindent
With \(\mathbb{A}'=\mathbb{A}\cup\{a_4,a_5\}\), the information in effect at \((\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}, \textrm{10:03})\) becomes \(\{a_2, a_3, a_5\}\) because \(a_4\) retracts \(a_1\), bringing \(a_5\) into effect instead.

\subsection{Reassertions and restatements}
\deffy{def. of a reassertion}{%
If a later assertion by the same positor is otherwise identical to an earlier one, the later is a \emph{reassertion} of the earlier.}{reassertion}

\deffy{def. of a restatement}{%
For two assertion posits \([\{(p,\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},c,T]\) and \([\{(p',\textit{posit}),(S,\textit{ascertains})\},c,T]\), with \(p=[D,v,t_{\mathrm{app}}]\) and \(p'=[D,v,t'_{\mathrm{app}}]\) such that \(t_{\mathrm{app}} < t'_{\mathrm{app}}\), the latter is a \emph{restatement} of the former.}{restatement}

\noindent
\textsc{Example (reassertion vs. restatement).} Let Charlie initially claim ``fluffy red'' at 10:00 with high confidence, then repeat the same claim on a later day (reassertion), and also apply the same confidence to the later appearance-time posit (restatement). Finally, Charlie downgrades confidence for the later appearance-time posit:
\begin{alignat*}{3}
p_{1} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{fluffy red},\;&& \textrm{10:00}]  \\
p_{4} = [\{(A, \textit{has beard})\},\;&& \textrm{fluffy red},\;&& \textrm{10:02}] \\
a_2 = [\{(p_{1}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
a_7 = [\{(p_{4}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}] \\
a_8 = [\{(p_{1}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.75,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}] \\
a_9 = [\{(p_{4}, \textit{posit}), (C, \textit{ascertains})\},\;&& 0.25,\;&& \textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03}]
\end{alignat*}
Here, \(a_8\) is a reassertion of \(a_2\) (only assertion time changes). Also, \(a_7\) is a restatement of \(a_2\) (same assertion time and value, later appearance time). The pair \(a_7\) and \(a_9\) illustrates belief revision without erasing history.

\subsection{Additional timelines as represented information}
\noindent
The formalism makes appearance time primitive on posits. When assertions are used, assertion time becomes a second temporal coordinate for that posit family. Other timelines that practical systems care about can be represented \emph{within} the formalism rather than added as new primitives.

\noindent
\textbf{Recording time.} When a database stores an assertion, that act of inscription can itself be represented by a further posit or assertion about the stored assertion. Recording is therefore not a third primitive axis of truth; it is metadata about when some posit or assertion entered a particular memory. In system terms, recording time may coincide with insertion time in the underlying engine, but conceptually it is still represented information about a representational act.

\noindent
\textbf{Structuring time.} Changes to classes, roles, types, and constraints are likewise representable as ordinary posits. If a class \textit{Price Cut} is introduced on one date and revised later, those changes have their own appearance times and assertions. The resulting history is a \emph{structuring timeline}: not a separate primitive clock, but the temporal evolution of model-level posits. This is one reason it matters that classes and related constructs live inside the same formalism.

\noindent
This perspective also clarifies a common implementation pitfall: when appearance, assertion, and recording times are not separately available, one may be used as a proxy for another. Such approximation may be operationally necessary, but it must not be mistaken for equivalence. Recording time is often a tolerable proxy for missing assertion time; it is a much riskier proxy for appearance time, since what entered the system \emph{now} may concern a state that held long before or will hold only in the future.

\section{Subsumption of Existing Models (Special Cases)}
\noindent
Traditional models can be seen as informal embeddings of transitional representation obtained by restricting: (i) the number of positors to one, (ii) certainty to \(c=1\), and (iii) temporal variation. The reductions below are illustrative rather than formal. Each claims that the \emph{intended semantics} of the named model can be expressed within transitional representation under the stated restrictions; they do not constitute proof that every query or constraint expressible in the source model has a mechanical translation.

\subsection{Third normal form, Anchor Modeling, Data Vault}
\noindent
\textsc{Third normal form (3NF).} A conventional relational row corresponds to posits whose appearance sets match the key of the row and whose values are treated as universally asserted: one implicit positor (the system of record), \(c=1\), and no retained assertion-time history \cite{Kent,Codd}. Updates are destructive in the sense that prior values are not representable without additional temporal machinery.

\noindent
\textsc{Anchor Modeling.} Anchors correspond to immutable unique identifiers, and attributes/ties correspond to posits with respectively unary and multi-appearance sets \cite{RonnbackEtAl}. The intended semantics of Anchor Modeling---a single institutional positor, certainty \(c=1\), all posits recorded from a fixed historical origin---can be \emph{embedded} in transitional representation by those restrictions. The embedding is informal: Anchor Modeling's historized attribute tables correspond to posit resolution $\rho$ over unary appearance sets, but Anchor Modeling's constraint layer (e.g.\ functionally dependent anchors) has no direct counterpart in the minimal class layer defined here. See also the Anchor Modeling reference site~\cite{Anchor}.

\noindent
\textsc{Data Vault.} Hubs correspond to immutable identifiers; satellites/links correspond to posits (unary vs. multi-appearance) but with emphasis on load-time tracking \cite{LinstedtEtAl,Hultgren}. In transitional representation, load time aligns most directly with \emph{recording} time: when a statement entered a particular store. It may sometimes be used as a proxy for assertion time when no better source timestamp exists, but the two should not be conflated. Appearance time remains the time of the modeled domain.

\subsection{Relationship to other paradigms}
\noindent
Restricting transitional representation further illuminates its relationship to other database paradigms. A traditional \emph{bitemporal database}~\cite{Snodgrass} corresponds to a single implicit positor with certainty \(c\in\{0,1\}\) only: either a fact is recorded or it is not. Extending certainty to the continuous interval \([0,1]\) recovers the behaviour of \emph{probabilistic databases}~\cite{BenjellounEtAl}, where confidence values weigh uncertain facts. The full signed range \(c\in[-1,1]\) additionally permits expressing certainty in a complement, an aspect absent from classical probabilistic models and closer to the uncertainty-theoretic treatment of~\cite{Liu}. Finally, lifting the single-positor restriction opens a \emph{multi-source} (multi-tenant) model in which competing beliefs from different actors coexist, are queryable independently, and are resolved only at query time.

\section{Comparison with RDF-based Formalisms}
\label{Sec:rdf}
\noindent
The Resource Description Framework (RDF)~\cite{RDF} and its extensions form the dominant standard for Semantic Web data. RDF's strengths---open-world semantics, universal URI-based identification, standardised query via SPARQL, and a rich ecosystem---are well established. The comparison below is therefore narrow: it examines only those points where transitional representation makes different primitives available, rather than arguing for replacement.

\subsection{The binary constraint and \(n\)-ary facts}
\noindent
An RDF triple $(s, p, o)$ is inherently binary: one predicate relates one subject to one object. A fact involving more participants must therefore introduce an intermediate node. The W3C note on n-ary relations~\cite{NoyRector} recommends exactly this pattern. The intermediate node is structurally useful, but it is still an artefact of binary encoding rather than the fact itself.

\noindent
By contrast, the same wedding fact is stated atomically in transitional representation:
\begin{align*}
\bigl[\{(H, \textit{is spouse}),\;(W, \textit{is spouse}),\;(M, \textit{officiated by}),\;(L, \textit{at location})\},\;\textit{married},\; t_{\mathrm{app}}\bigr]
\end{align*}
Any number of positors may assert and revise certainty about this single posit without introducing an event node, because the \emph{appearance set} is itself the composite subject.

\subsection{Annotating statements and sources}
\noindent
RDF offers three main ways to attach metadata such as provenance, certainty, and time to a claim: classic reification, named graphs, and RDF-star~\cite{CarrollEtAl,HartigChampin,NoyRector}. Reification creates a separate \texttt{rdf:Statement} resource; named graphs annotate a graph name; RDF-star embeds one triple inside another. These differ syntactically, but they share one limitation relevant here: the metadata is attached by additional conventions rather than carried by a single primitive that already includes both \emph{what was claimed}, \emph{who claimed it}, and \emph{when it was claimed}.

\noindent
RDF-star is the closest analogue, since it operates at triple granularity. In the running example, Charlie's belief about Archie's beard could be written:
\begin{align*}
\langle\!\langle A\;\texttt{ex:hasBeard}\;\textit{fluffy red}\rangle\!\rangle\;\texttt{ex:certainty}\;0.75, \qquad
\langle\!\langle A\;\texttt{ex:hasBeard}\;\textit{fluffy red}\rangle\!\rangle\;\texttt{ex:assertedAt}\;\textit{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}
\end{align*}
The embedded triple, however, still has no slot for appearance time. Encoding that requires a further annotation property (e.g.\ \texttt{ex:validFrom~10:00}), and different positors with different views of appearance time again require extra conventions or event-node patterns.

\subsection{The temporal gap: bitemporality}
\noindent
OWL-Time~\cite{OWLTime} provides a rich ontology for temporal instants, intervals, and ordering relations~\cite{Allen}. It is well suited to representing when a state holds in the world (\emph{appearance time}, or valid time in database terminology). What it does not provide is a standard account of \emph{when a source's belief about that state was recorded or revised}. Assertion time---the second temporal axis of transitional representation---has no standard counterpart in RDF or OWL-Time.

\noindent
Systems that need both axes must therefore add custom ``valid-from / transaction-time'' conventions on top of RDF. Those conventions are application-specific, making bitemporal history portable only at the application layer. Transitional representation instead makes appearance time part of every posit and assertion time part of every assertion.

\subsection{Conflict handling and uniformity}
\noindent
Wikidata~\cite{VrandecicKrotzsch}, the world's largest public knowledge graph, resolves competing values operationally through statement ranks. This is effective for editorial workflows, but it pushes conflict resolution toward write time: one value is marked preferred, another normal, another deprecated. Transitional representation instead preserves competing assertions and applies no built-in fusion policy beyond the bitemporal \emph{information in effect} selection.

\noindent
A second difference is uniformity. In transitional representation, assertions are themselves posits, so the same representational machinery applies recursively. Annotating an assertion therefore uses the same constructs as annotating a first-order fact. RDF can express nested annotation patterns, especially with RDF-star, but the recursion is a matter of increasingly elaborate encoding rather than a single repeated primitive.

\section{Related Work}

\noindent
\textbf{Temporal databases.}
Distinctions between the time of an event and the time at which it is recorded have long been recognized~\cite{Reichenbach} and are central to temporal database theory~\cite{Snodgrass}. Modern SQL provides partial temporal features~\cite{SQL2011}, but these remain insufficient for multi-source disagreement~\cite{GolfarelliRizzi,Johnston}. Johnston~\cite{Johnston} also introduces a speech-act framing (with actors resembling positors) but does not address signed uncertainty.

\noindent
\textbf{Uncertainty and provenance.}
\cite{BenjellounEtAl} introduces databases with uncertainty and lineage, with confidence values analogous to signed certainty and lineage analogous to positors, but without temporality. \cite{DyllaEtAl} extends the model with valid time but does not represent transitions in the opinions of sources over assertion time. Signed certainty is related to the belief degree of~\cite{Liu}, whose normality and duality axioms correspond to our non-contradiction and bounded properties, albeit with a symmetry around $0.5$ rather than $0$. Readers from the Dempster-Shafer tradition will recognise structural parallels: over a two-element frame $\{v, \neg v\}$, the bounded property $c + d \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$ mirrors the Dempster-Shafer relationship between belief and plausibility ($\mathrm{Bel}(v) + \mathrm{Bel}(\neg v) \leq 1$, with equality when the mass of the full frame is zero), and $c = 0$ corresponds to total ignorance ($m(\{v, \neg v\}) = 1$). The key difference is that signed certainty is assigned per positor and may be asserted about any posit at any assertion time, whereas basic belief assignment is a static, source-global function over the power set of possibilities. Surveys of imprecision and uncertainty in database systems can be found in~\cite{Smets,Motro}.

\noindent
\textbf{Write-time vs.\ query-time conflict resolution.}
A central principle of this paper is that resolving conflicts at write time is lossy~\cite{MassaroEtAl}: the integration rules must be fixed in advance, are assumed perfect, and cannot be revised retrospectively. Moving resolution to query time, without discarding any source assertion, is advocated in~\cite{LinEtAl} in the context of merging databases under constraints. The present formalism operationalises this principle through temporal resolution, of which the global \emph{information in effect} is the simplest case. The information-fusion literature~\cite{SmetsEtAl,LiuEtAl} has explored real-time integration of uncertain sensory data; transitional representation offers an alternative in which raw source assertions are preserved and conflict resolution is deferred to decision time. This deferral principle also parallels the CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) and event-sourcing architectural pattern~\cite{Fowler2011}, in which write-side commands are stored as immutable events and read-side projections are derived on demand. The shared intuition is that early commitment to a derived view forecloses later reinterpretation; transitional representation extends this principle to the level of the representation formalism itself.

\noindent
\textbf{Linguistics and epistemics.}
\cite{Papafragou} establishes that ``subjective epistemic modality is time-dependent'': vague assertions are more prone to revision, a dynamic that our assertion-time axis tracks explicitly. \cite{MoensSteedman} define \emph{culmination} as a point in time marking a transition to a new state of the world, closely paralleling our use of appearance time to capture transitions between states.

\noindent
\textbf{Temporal knowledge graphs.}
Temporal knowledge graph (TKG) embedding methods associate timestamps or time intervals with triples in a knowledge graph and learn representations capturing temporal patterns~\cite{Lacroix2020}. This line of work addresses \emph{inferring} missing facts at unobserved times rather than representing provenance and certainty of source-supplied assertions. Transitional representation is complementary: it defines a storage and query semantics over contested, evolving assertions; TKG embeddings can operate over the resolved snapshot produced from such a store. The posit-level temporal resolution operator $\rho$ is precisely what converts a transitional store into the time-slice that TKG methods typically consume.

\noindent
\textbf{Property graphs.}
The property graph model, recently standardised as ISO/IEC GQL~\cite{GQL}, supports per-edge key--value properties and has seen wide industry adoption (e.g.\ Neo4j, openCypher~\cite{Francis}). Properties can carry provenance annotations and numeric weights. However, like RDF, there is no standard per-edge bitemporal axis, no built-in signed certainty range, and no non-destructive retraction primitive. Where property graphs go further than the RDF family is in treating edge identity as a first-class feature, which is conceptually close to the posit identity used here. Translating a binary posit to a property-graph edge is natural; $n$-ary posits again require an intermediate node.

\noindent
\textbf{Graph and RDF formalisms.} Section~\ref{Sec:rdf} provides a focused comparison with RDF-based annotation mechanisms (classic reification, named graphs, RDF-star~\cite{HartigChampin}) and the Wikidata knowledge graph~\cite{VrandecicKrotzsch}. At the level of this overview: an RDF triple~\cite{RDF} is binary and atemporal by design; RDF-star and property graphs can attach per-triple provenance and time but provide no standard bitemporal axis, no signed certainty, and no uniform retraction semantics. Transitional representation makes $n$-ary appearance sets, bitemporality, signed certainty, and non-destructive retraction primitive, so that conflict resolution can be deferred uniformly to query time. RDF-star was incorporated into RDF~1.2 during a W3C Working Group process begun in 2023; this standardisation does not add the built-in bitemporality or per-positor signed certainty noted as absent in Table~\ref{Table:rdfcomparison}.

\section{A Query Language Sketch: Traqula}
\label{Sec:traqula}
\noindent
The reference implementation Positorium~\cite{Positorium} exposes a domain-specific query and mutation language called Traqula. The grammar below (abridged from the formal PEG grammar) captures its essential structure:
\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{tabular}{r l}
$\textit{script}$ & $\to$ $(\textit{add\_role} \mid \textit{add\_posit} \mid \textit{search})^*$ \\
$\textit{add\_role}$ & $\to$ \texttt{add role} $\textit{role}^+$ \texttt{;} \\
$\textit{add\_posit}$ & $\to$ \texttt{add posit} $\textit{posit}^+$ \texttt{;} \\
$\textit{search}$ & $\to$ \texttt{search} $\textit{pattern}^+$ $\textit{where}?$ $\textit{return}$ $\textit{limit}?$ \texttt{;} \\
$\textit{posit}$ & $\to$ $\textit{name}?$\; \texttt{[}\;\textit{appset},\;\textit{value},\;\textit{time}\;\texttt{]} \\
$\textit{pattern}$ & $\to$ $\textit{name}?$\; \texttt{[}\;\textit{appset},\;\textit{value},\;\textit{time}\;\texttt{]}\; \textit{as\_of}?$ \\
$\textit{as\_of}$ & $\to$ \texttt{as of} $(\textit{time} \mid \textit{variable} \mid$ \texttt{@NOW}$)$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{quote}
Variables bind identities ({\small\texttt{+var}} creates and binds a new identity, {\small\texttt{var}} recalls a bound one), while {\small\texttt{*}} is a wildcard that matches without binding. In a multi-pattern search, variable bindings are shared across patterns, so a binding established in one clause constrains the next.

\noindent
The \texttt{as of} clause is the direct syntactic realization of posit resolution $\rho(\mathbb{P}, t_{@})$ (Definition~\ref{Def:resolution}): it reduces the matching posits for each appearance set to the single latest one at or before the given time, implementing a snapshot query. Omitting \texttt{as of} returns the full history. Multiple patterns in one \texttt{search} may carry independent \texttt{as of} clauses whose cut points can refer to variables bound by earlier patterns, realising the data-dependent stratified cuts described in Section~\ref{Sec:rdf}.

\noindent
\textsc{Example 1: state at a point in time.} The query below returns Archie's beard state as it would be reported using posit-level temporal resolution at \texttt{10:01}---the value of the latest posit for that appearance set at or before the cut:
\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{verbatim}
search [{(archie, has beard)}, +v, *] as of '10:01'
return v;
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
Because the latest posit for \(\{(A,\textit{has beard})\}\) at \texttt{10:01} carries the value \textit{fluffy red} (the \texttt{10:02} shaved-clean posit is not yet visible), the query returns \textit{fluffy red}. Without \texttt{as of} the query returns the full history.

\noindent
\textsc{Example 2: information in effect (assertion resolution).} The formal operator $\mathbb{A}(T_{@}, t_{@})$ is two-dimensional: it takes an assertion-time cut $T_{@}$ and an appearance-time cut $t_{@}$ independently. In Traqula, the assertion-time axis is controlled by the \texttt{as of} clause on the assertion pattern, while the appearance-time axis is controlled by the \texttt{as of} clause on the inner domain posit pattern (or left open to return the full history of posit states). The query below retrieves all currently effective assertions without restricting appearance time:
\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{verbatim}
search [{(+p, posit), (+src, ascertains)}, +c, *] as of @NOW
return src, c;
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The reserved roles \textit{posit} and \textit{ascertains} identify assertion posits. The \texttt{as of @NOW} clause fixes the assertion-time cut $T_{@}$; the wildcard \texttt{*} on the assertion-time position means all visible assertion times are retained. To also restrict appearance time, a second pattern matching the inner posit \texttt{p} with its own \texttt{as of} clause would be added.

\noindent
\textsc{Example 3: data-dependent dual-timepoint join.} The most distinctive idiom of the query language is using a posit's own appearance time as the temporal condition on which assertions about it are visible. This directly realises the two-dimensional cut: the appearance time of a domain posit gates the assertion-time window applied when querying beliefs about that posit. The query below retrieves Archie's current class memberships and, for each one, finds only the assertions that were already on record as of the date that classification started:
\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{verbatim}
search +p [{(archie, thing), (+cls, class)}, +status, +at] as of @NOW,
          [{(p, posit), (+src, ascertains)}, +c, *] as of at
where c >= 50%
return cls, status, at, src, c;
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The prefix \texttt{+p} names the classifier posit. The variable \texttt{at} captures its appearance time --- the date the classification started. The second pattern finds assertion posits about \texttt{p}; \texttt{as of at} applies \texttt{at} as the assertion-time ceiling, so only assertions whose assertion time does not exceed \texttt{at} are visible. In the running example, Archie's \textit{Suspect} classification ($q_4$) starts on 2024-03-01, and the investigator $I$ asserted it on the same day with certainty $0.75$; that assertion is therefore returned. A retroactive endorsement added on 2024-03-03 would only appear in a query cutting at 2024-03-03 or later. The query thereby distinguishes positors who vouched for a classification contemporaneously from those who did so in hindsight --- a forensically natural question in the investigation scenario. The variable \texttt{at} operates simultaneously as a time coordinate in the first pattern and as the assertion-time ceiling in the second, realising both dimensions of $\mathbb{A}(T_{@}, t_{@})$ from a single bound value.

\noindent
\textsc{Example 4: filtering by certainty and time.} The \texttt{where} clause supports filtering on bound variables using comparators. To find all beard-related assertions with positive certainty recorded on or after a given day:
\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{verbatim}
search [+p {(archie, has beard)}, +v, +t],
       [{(p, posit), (+src, ascertains)}, +c, +T]
where c >= 75% and T >= '2024-03-01'
return src, v, t, T, c;
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
The first pattern names the posit \texttt{p} and binds the value and appearance time; the second uses \texttt{p} to locate the assertions about it and filters by certainty and assertion time. This illustrates how the two layers---domain posits and assertion posits---are queried through shared variable bindings rather than by a separate join primitive.

\noindent
These four examples cover the principal idioms: snapshot queries, assertion-layer queries with the two-dimensional cut made explicit, the dual-timepoint join in which a posit's appearance time gates assertion visibility, and cross-layer joins with certainty filtering. The full language includes constant tokens \texttt{@BOT} (beginning of time) and \texttt{@EOT} (end of time) for open-ended range queries, JSON and decimal value types, and a \texttt{limit} clause; details are in the Positorium repository.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\noindent
Transitional representation treats concurrency, uncertainty, and bitemporality as aspects of one representation construct: the posit. Because even a minimal class layer is expressible in the same terms, the formalism can represent not only contested facts but contested \emph{classifications}. Temporal resolution enables query-time conflict handling without destructive updates, whether a query uses one global cut or several clause-local ones. The formalism prescribes no fusion strategy: how competing assertions are ultimately reconciled --- whether by selecting the highest positive certainty, by credibility-weighted aggregation, by recency, or by any domain-specific policy --- remains a consumer decision made at query time and left outside the representation layer. At that point the consumer receives the relevant resolved slice or slices: every surviving assertion with its positor identity, signed certainty, appearance time, and value. To illustrate: if the information in effect at \((\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}03},\,10{:}03)\) contains \(a_2=[\{(p_1,\textit{posit}),(C,\textit{ascertains})\},0.75,\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]\) and \(a_3=[\{(p_3,\textit{posit}),(C,\textit{ascertains})\},-1.0,\textrm{2024\text{-}03\text{-}01}]\) for the appearance set \(\{(A,\textit{has beard})\}\), a \emph{max-certainty} policy selects Charlie's value (highest positive certainty); a \emph{recency-biased} policy may weight Bella's later corroboration more heavily once \(a_5\) arrives; an \emph{agreement-seeking} policy might flag the appearance set as contested and decline to surface any single value. All three are valid consumer choices over the same unmodified slice.

\noindent
The same representational machinery also extends beyond descriptive facts. Normative claims such as obligations, permissions, and prohibitions can be represented as posits and disputed through assertions; semantic mapping claims such as synonymy, code translation, or partial equivalence between vocabularies can likewise be represented and revised over time; and evidential support can be attached uniformly by asserting how a belief was formed (e.g.\ eyewitness report, sensor reading, laboratory result, or model output). These are recurring integration problems in regulated, multilingual, and data-fusion settings, and they require no new primitives beyond those introduced here.

\noindent
This system perspective clarifies the motivation further. Traditional databases tend to require conformance before storage; schema-light systems often postpone it entirely and inconsistently to read time. Transitional representation occupies an intermediate position. It allows raw competing assertions to be stored without forced reconciliation, while already making classifications available as represented, evolving, and source-dependent information. The same design points toward richer schema- and constraint-level representations. In that sense, the formalism suggests a path toward \emph{eventual conformance}: the possibility that structure itself emerges, changes, and is disputed over time. Operationally this means that a class definition, a role constraint, or a co-reference claim can enter the store as an asserted posit, attract agreement or disagreement from other positors over time, and progressively become the effective schema --- not by administrative fiat, but through the same bitemporal resolution that governs any other piece of information.

\noindent
A reference implementation named Positorium~\cite{Positorium} demonstrates the feasibility of the approach. Positorium is an experimental database engine written in Rust~\cite{Matsakis} that stores data as posits and exposes Traqula, a query and mutation language whose core idioms are described in Section~\ref{Sec:traqula}.

\noindent
Future work includes richer typing beyond the minimal class layer formalized here, model bootstrapping within the representation itself, constraint evaluation (e.g., higher-order cardinality) over indecisive bodies of information, fuller treatment of recording and structuring histories in implementations, and empirical evaluation of storage and query strategies for large bodies of assertions. Identification --- the process of establishing which identifier to use --- remains outside the representation layer as a deliberate design boundary: it is a subjective process, of which key-based lookup is only a reproducible special case under prior agreement.
